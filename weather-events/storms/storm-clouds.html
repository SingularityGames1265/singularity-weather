Storm clouds

This page features all clouds associated with storms.

Cumulonimbus clouds

Cumulonimbus clouds are the most common form of storm cloud. Their name comes from the Latin words cumulus (heap) and nimbus (rain).

They form when the surface is heated. Usually a heatwave preceeds this formation, making them a common sight in the summer months. These clouds start of as regular cumulus clouds, but these can start growing incredibly tall due to hot and humid air. This means that they can extend up to the top of the troposphere.

Due to the build up of moisture and pressure, they tend to release a variety of precipitation. this includes rain, hail and rarely snow. Also because of the pressure, these clouds store a large amount of kinetic energy. The average cumulonimbus cloud stores about the same amount of energy as ten Hiroshima-sized bombs. This energy is discharged with lightning.

When the cloud meets the tropopause, it starts extending horizontally, creating an anvil shape. This is one of the defining features to tell if it is a cumulonimbus cloud. Rarely, if a storm is incredibly strong, the main tower can extend a bit above the anvil. This is called an overshooting top.

Sometimes, multiple of these clouds can merge into something larger like a squall or supercell.

Asperitas clouds

Asperitas clouds are an accesory cloud. They usually appear before and after strong storms. Asperitas means roughness in Latin. They are currently the newest recognised cloud, meaing little is known about them and how they form.

They look like the bottom of ocean waves, but up in the sky instead.

Mammatus clouds

Mammatus clouds are also an accesory cloud. Present during heavy storms, they usually form under the anvil of the main cloud. Mammatus is derived from the Latin word mamma meaning udder or breast.

Under the anvil, sacks of cold air are formed, these can create dips in the main anvil cloud, resulting in cell-like structures.
